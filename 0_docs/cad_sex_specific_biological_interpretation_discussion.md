# Biological Interpretation and Discussion

## Global GWAS Architecture of CAD

The sex-specific GWAS analyses revealed significant genetic association signals for coronary artery disease (CAD) in both males and females, supporting the polygenic nature of CAD susceptibility. Manhattan plot analysis demonstrated multiple genomic loci exceeding the genome-wide significance threshold in both datasets, while QQ plots showed clear deviations from the null expectation line in the upper tail of the distributions, suggesting enrichment of true biological association signals rather than random statistical variation alone.

The male GWAS analysis appeared to exhibit stronger and broader association peaks across multiple chromosomes compared with the female analysis. In particular, prominent signals were observed on chromosomes 6 and 9. Chromosome 9 is especially relevant in CAD biology because the 9p21 region is among the most consistently validated cardiovascular risk loci reported in previous GWAS studies. Variants within this region have been associated with vascular remodeling, smooth muscle cell regulation, endothelial dysfunction, and atherosclerotic plaque progression.

Strong association signals on chromosome 6 may additionally indicate involvement of immune-related genomic regions located within the major histocompatibility complex (MHC). These findings are consistent with the inflammatory and immune-related enrichment patterns identified throughout the male-specific analyses.

The Venn diagram analyses demonstrated both shared and sex-specific genetic architectures. At the SNP level, males showed a substantially larger number of significant variants compared with females, while a subset of SNPs remained shared between both sexes. Interestingly, overlap increased considerably at the gene level, suggesting that distinct SNPs may converge on common biological pathways and molecular functions.

Overall, these findings support the hypothesis that CAD involves both conserved and sex-dependent genetic mechanisms contributing to disease susceptibility.

# Shared Molecular Mechanisms Underlying CAD

The shared hub genes and pathway enrichment analyses identified several conserved biological mechanisms potentially contributing to CAD pathophysiology in both males and females. The strongest shared biological themes involved lipid metabolism, cholesterol transport, vascular signaling, endothelial dysfunction, extracellular matrix remodeling, and atherosclerotic progression.

Several highly connected hub genes occupied central positions within the shared interaction network, including AKT1, APOE, APOB, FN1, SMAD3, LPL, RELA, and RHOA. These genes participate in multiple interconnected cardiovascular processes and may represent core components of CAD molecular architecture.

AKT1 demonstrated the highest network connectivity and strong betweenness centrality, suggesting a potentially important role in vascular signaling and endothelial homeostasis. AKT1 is part of the PI3K-AKT signaling pathway, which regulates endothelial survival, nitric oxide production, inflammatory signaling, and vascular remodeling. Its central network position may therefore reflect conserved signaling mechanisms underlying CAD progression in both sexes.

Lipid-related hub genes including APOE, APOB, and LPL strongly support the importance of cholesterol transport and lipoprotein metabolism in CAD biology. APOE and APOB are major structural and functional components of circulating lipoprotein particles, whereas LPL regulates triglyceride metabolism and lipid clearance. Dysregulation of these pathways contributes directly to lipid accumulation, foam-cell formation, and atherosclerotic plaque development.

The shared pathway enrichment analysis further reinforced the central role of lipid dysregulation. Significant enrichment was observed for pathways related to cholesterol metabolism, plasma lipoprotein particles, lipoprotein particle organization, acylglycerol metabolic processes, and statin inhibition of cholesterol production. These findings are highly consistent with established CAD pathophysiology and support the biological validity of the identified shared gene set.

Extracellular matrix remodeling and vascular structural regulation were additionally represented through genes such as FN1 and SMAD3. FN1 encodes fibronectin, an important extracellular matrix protein involved in cell adhesion and vascular remodeling, while SMAD3 participates in TGF-β signaling pathways associated with fibrosis and arterial structural adaptation.

Inflammatory regulation was also represented by RELA and RHOA. RELA is a major component of the NF-κB inflammatory signaling pathway and is involved in immune activation and endothelial inflammatory responses. RHOA contributes to cytoskeletal organization and vascular smooth muscle cell dynamics.

The shared pathway enrichment dotplot demonstrated strong enrichment for pathways associated with cholesterol metabolism, atherosclerosis, lipid regulation, and biological process regulation. These enrichments suggest that lipid dysregulation and vascular dysfunction represent conserved CAD mechanisms across sexes.

Overall, the shared analyses indicate that CAD susceptibility in both males and females converges on core biological processes involving lipid metabolism, endothelial dysfunction, vascular remodeling, and atherosclerotic progression.

# Male-Specific CAD Molecular Architecture

The male-specific analyses revealed a molecular profile strongly enriched for inflammatory signaling, cytokine-mediated communication, intracellular signaling cascades, vascular stress responses, and immune activation. Multiple independent analyses consistently supported the presence of a more inflammation-dominated CAD architecture in males.

The male-specific interaction network appeared substantially denser and more interconnected compared with the female network, suggesting stronger coordination between signaling pathways and immune-regulatory mechanisms. Several tightly connected clusters were observed within the network, indicating extensive communication between inflammatory mediators, intracellular signaling proteins, and vascular-response pathways.

Among the male-specific hub genes, STAT3 demonstrated the highest connectivity and betweenness centrality. STAT3 is a major transcription factor involved in cytokine signaling, inflammatory activation, endothelial dysfunction, and vascular remodeling. Its prominent network position may indicate a central regulatory role in male-associated CAD biology.

IL1B was another major inflammatory hub identified in the male network. IL1B encodes interleukin-1β, a pro-inflammatory cytokine strongly implicated in atherosclerotic plaque formation, immune-cell recruitment, endothelial activation, and vascular inflammation. The identification of IL1B as a highly connected hub further supports the importance of inflammatory signaling pathways in male CAD susceptibility.

Additional signaling-related hub genes included PLCG1, NRAS, GNB1, and GNAI3, which participate in intracellular signal transduction, vascular stress responses, and inflammatory pathway activation. These findings suggest extensive dysregulation of interconnected signaling networks in males.

The male pathway enrichment analyses strongly reinforced this inflammatory and signaling-driven profile. Significant enrichment was observed for pathways related to intracellular signal transduction, regulation of signaling, cytokine-mediated signaling, chemokine signaling, MAPK signaling, Ras signaling, response to stress, and immune system processes.

The enrichment of the Fluid shear stress and atherosclerosis pathway is particularly important in the context of CAD biology. Disturbed vascular shear stress is known to promote endothelial dysfunction, inflammatory activation, and atherosclerotic plaque development. This finding may therefore indicate altered mechanotransduction and vascular stress responses in male CAD susceptibility.

The male enrichment dotplot visually demonstrated strong overrepresentation of signaling, cellular communication, and stimulus-response pathways. Compared with the female enrichment profile, males exhibited stronger emphasis on inflammatory signaling and vascular stress-response mechanisms.

The male QQ plot additionally showed stronger and earlier deviation from the null expectation line compared with females, suggesting a broader and more pronounced polygenic association architecture. Combined with the dense interaction network and extensive pathway enrichment, these findings collectively support a model in which male CAD susceptibility is characterized by amplified inflammatory and intracellular signaling activity.

Overall, the male-specific analyses suggest that immune activation, cytokine signaling, endothelial dysfunction, vascular stress responses, and intracellular communication pathways may contribute prominently to CAD pathophysiology in males.

# Female-Specific CAD Molecular Architecture

In contrast to the male-specific analyses, the female-specific molecular profile appeared more strongly associated with transcriptional regulation, metabolic adaptation, mitochondrial function, intracellular homeostasis, and regulatory cellular processes.

The female interaction network appeared less densely connected and more modular compared with the male network. Smaller interconnected clusters and fewer highly connected inflammatory regions were observed, potentially indicating a more distributed regulatory architecture.

Among the female-specific hub genes, PPARGC1A demonstrated notably high betweenness centrality. PPARGC1A encodes PGC-1α, a major regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis and cellular energy metabolism. This gene has been associated with oxidative metabolism, endothelial regulation, mitochondrial homeostasis, and cardiovascular energy balance. Its central network position may therefore indicate an important role for metabolic and mitochondrial regulation in female CAD susceptibility.

JAK2 and STAT2 represented important inflammatory and cytokine-signaling components within the female network, suggesting that immune regulation remains biologically relevant in females. However, compared with males, the female profile appeared less dominated by extensive inflammatory signaling cascades.

The identification of LEP and POMC additionally suggested involvement of endocrine and metabolic regulatory pathways. LEP encodes leptin, a hormone involved in adipose signaling, inflammation, vascular function, and energy homeostasis. These findings may indicate stronger contributions from metabolic and hormonal regulatory processes in female CAD biology.

Several mitochondrial and cellular respiration-related genes, including CS, NDUFS3, MRPL17, and HSPA9, were also identified among the female-specific hubs. These genes are involved in oxidative phosphorylation, mitochondrial energy production, and cellular stress adaptation. Their enrichment may reflect altered mitochondrial function and metabolic homeostasis in female-associated CAD mechanisms.

The female pathway enrichment analyses demonstrated strong enrichment for pathways related to regulation of DNA-templated transcription, RNA biosynthetic processes, RNA metabolism, transcriptional regulation, lysosomal activity, transport vesicle membranes, and intracellular organization.

The female pathway enrichment dotplot further supported this profile, highlighting strong enrichment for broad regulatory and intracellular processes rather than dominant inflammatory signaling pathways. Compared with the male enrichment profile, the female analyses appeared more strongly centered on gene-expression regulation, intracellular homeostasis, and metabolic adaptation.

The female Manhattan and QQ plots nevertheless demonstrated significant association signals, particularly on chromosomes 6 and 9, indicating that biologically meaningful CAD-associated loci are also present in females. However, the overall association architecture appeared less extensive than that observed in males.

Overall, the female-specific analyses suggest that CAD susceptibility in females may involve stronger contributions from transcriptional regulation, mitochondrial activity, metabolic adaptation, intracellular organization, and endocrine-related pathways.

# Sex-Specific Differences in CAD Biology

Direct comparison of the male and female analyses revealed substantial sex-dependent differences in CAD-associated molecular architecture.

The male CAD profile appeared strongly dominated by inflammatory signaling, cytokine-mediated communication, vascular stress responses, immune activation, and intracellular signaling cascades. Males additionally demonstrated denser interaction networks, stronger QQ plot deviations, broader Manhattan plot association peaks, and extensive enrichment of signaling-related pathways.

By contrast, the female CAD profile appeared more strongly associated with transcriptional regulation, mitochondrial function, intracellular homeostasis, and metabolic regulation. Female networks were less densely interconnected and demonstrated greater enrichment for regulatory and metabolic pathways rather than highly inflammatory signaling signatures.

Despite these differences, both sexes shared several conserved biological mechanisms centered around lipid metabolism, cholesterol transport, endothelial dysfunction, and atherosclerotic progression. Shared hub genes such as APOE, APOB, LPL, and AKT1 support the presence of core vascular and lipid-associated mechanisms contributing to CAD susceptibility across sexes.

The larger overlap observed at the gene level compared with the SNP level further suggests that distinct genetic variants may converge on common biological pathways and molecular processes. This observation supports the concept of functional convergence in CAD pathophysiology.

Collectively, these findings suggest that while lipid dysregulation and vascular dysfunction represent common CAD mechanisms, sex-specific biological differences may influence how inflammatory, metabolic, and regulatory pathways contribute to disease susceptibility and progression.

# Overall Biological Interpretation and Conclusion

This integrated sex-specific GWAS and network biology analysis identified both shared and sex-dependent molecular mechanisms potentially contributing to coronary artery disease susceptibility.

The analyses consistently demonstrated that CAD is a highly polygenic disorder involving complex interactions between lipid metabolism, endothelial dysfunction, inflammatory signaling, vascular remodeling, and intracellular regulatory pathways.

Shared analyses highlighted the central importance of cholesterol metabolism, lipoprotein regulation, vascular signaling, and atherosclerotic processes across both sexes. Hub genes such as APOE, APOB, LPL, AKT1, and FN1 occupied central positions within the shared interaction network and may represent conserved components of CAD molecular architecture.

Male-specific analyses suggested stronger involvement of inflammatory and signaling-driven mechanisms, including cytokine signaling, chemokine pathways, MAPK signaling, vascular stress responses, and immune activation. Dense interaction networks and strong GWAS signal enrichment further supported the presence of amplified inflammatory molecular activity in males.

In contrast, female-specific analyses suggested stronger contributions from transcriptional regulation, mitochondrial activity, metabolic adaptation, intracellular homeostasis, and endocrine-related processes. These findings may indicate a more regulatory and metabolism-centered molecular architecture underlying female CAD susceptibility.

Overall, this study supports the hypothesis that CAD involves both conserved vascular-lipid mechanisms and sex-specific biological processes. These findings may contribute to improved understanding of sex-dependent CAD pathophysiology and may help guide future investigations into personalized cardiovascular risk assessment and therapeutic strategies.
